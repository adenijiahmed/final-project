Tanzania Travel Guide

Islands of Zanzibar and Pemba

The current population of Tanzania is approximately 51 million People comprised of indigenous people and sub-populations of other origins such as Indians, Arabs and Europeans. Tanzania culture is a delightful mix of influences with over 120 tribes. Tanzania is one of the most culturally diverse countries in the world. From the tall graceful Maasai warriors, the ancient ways of the Hadza bushmen, the resourceful agricultural practices of the Meru, the artistic talents of the Makonde to the Chaga farmers and traders. Each of the 120 different tribes in Tanzania have their own distinct ways of life but together, they gracefully unite to form Tanzania. Zanzibar on the other hand, has a blended culture from Shirazi (Persia), Arabs, the Comorians (Comoro), and the Bantu from the Mainland. The Island of the locals pretty much determines what tribe they belong to. Waunguja would emanate from Unguja Island, Wapemba from Pemba Island, and Watumbatu from Tumbato Island.

Language

A Sukuma village some time between 1906 and 1918

Over 120 languages are spoken in Tanzania, most of them from the Bantu family. The largest is Sukuma, with others including the Nyamwezi, Makonde, Nyakyusa, and the Chaga of the Kilimanjaro.

Despite the eminent diversity and plurality of the tribes, almost all Tanzanians are fluent with the National and Official Language Swahili (Kiswahili). The second official language is English, which is also the business language. Most Tanzanians with post-secondary education speak both official languages fluently in addition to their tribal languages. Most people identify themselves as Tanzanians first and foremost, reflecting the ideals, which were introduced by Mwl. Nyerere, the First Leader of the Nation and further strengthened by the successive generations of leaderships where Swahili was prioritized over local languages and indeed has been an instrumental tool for National unity

Religion

Christians and Muslims communities in Tanzania are approximately equal in size, each accounting to 30-40% of the population, with the remainder consisting of Traditional religions. In the case of Zanzibar, almost 97% of the population practices Islamic faith the remaining mix is a combination of Hindu and Christians.

With large communities of both Christians and Muslims, it is common for towns to have a mosque and church close-by or even next to each other. In addition, festivals/ holidays of both religions are given equal recognition, and no serious friction between people with different group/ religions has been prevalent.

The Nomads

Maasai Herdsman

In Northern Tanzania some groups speak Khoisan or “Click-sound” languages such as Sandawe & Hadza. Some speak Nilotic languages such as Maasai. These Nomads have been keeping their tradition and culture, herding cattle across large areas and living off the animals’ milk, blood and meat.

The Maasai are particularly known for their distinctive dress and the warrior-status of their men. Males go through a number of stages, from junior warrior to senior elders.

Kind and Polite

From a young age, Tanzanian children are taught to be polite and respectful. They will normally greet their elders with the phrase “Shikamoo” literally translates as “ I hold your feet”.

Due to the sense of brotherhood fostered by founders of the Nation, adults will frequently address strangers as Dada (sister) or Kaka (brother) or alternatively as ndugu (comrade or relative).

The Food

Ugali Served with Beans

Tanzanian cuisine is unique and widely varied. The coastal region cuisine is characterized with spicy foods and use of coconut milk. Such foods are; Pilau(wild rice/ mixed rice), Bagia, Biryani, kabab, Kashata(coconut or groundnuts rolls), Sambusa(Samosa). When you are in Zanzibar on the other hand, and the wind blows in the right direction, the fragrance of spice is deliciously strong and you know you are in Zanzibar, the Spice Island, with its cloves, and cinnamon, lichee nuts, cocoa beans and coconut.

As you move inland you will find foods that are less spicy; Wali(rice), Ugali, Chapati(a wheat pressed bread), Kuku choma(grilled chicken), Nyama Choma(grilled meat), Nyama pori(wild/ bush meat that is either sun dried, grilled or cooked), Kiti Moto(grilled pork), Mishikaki(skewed meat), Samaki(fish), Ndizi(Plantains/ bananas), Bamia(Okra), Mchicha( greens/ spinach), Njegere(peas), Maharage(Beans), Kisamvu(cassava leaves), kisusio(soup from boiled animal bones and meat or blood) and many dishes prepared the Tanzanian way.

Maandazi

Famous Snacks include; Maandazi (bread-like rolls), Visheti, Kashata (coconut or groundnuts rolls), Kebab, Sambusa (Samosa), Mkate wa kumimina, Vileja, Vitumbua (rice cakes) ,Bagia, Firigisi (grilled gizzards), Tende (dates), Korosho, karanga ( groundnuts), Daga (fried nut-sized fish), Senene (pan grilled grasshoppers), kumbikumbi (pan grilled …) and many others.

Native beverages include; Chai (tea ) which is usually a breakfast beverage taken with Chapati, Maandazi, Mkate(breads), Mayai(eggs). Kahawa (coffee), is another famous beverage, which is more commonly taken in the evenings, when the sun is cool and people are on the front porch, playing cards, Bao or just chatting. Many people drink coffee with Kashata (coconut or groundnuts rolls).

music

Siti Binti Saad

The music industry in Tanzania stretches from Traditional African Music to a distinctive hip-hop genre known as Bongo-flava (blend of all sorts of melodies, beats, rhythms and sounds). This industry has evolved over the years, and the native music is morphing into new music that is a combination of the old, with the new and imported sounds and rhythms. With that mix of influences and the original feel of local music Tanzania musicians are among the best in Eastern Africa region.

also Traditional music is also unique reflecting diverse traditions and culture across the country, with distinctive traditional instruments, many of which are specific to particular ethnic groups. Likewise, in recent years, some Artists have blended to bring in some imported/ modern flavors. Nevertheless, the traditional music in Tanzania has continued to be distinctive, with strong sense of ownership and cultural facsimile. Some of famous traditional music blends include: Taarab, Lizombe, Mchiriku and Mduara. All these by large, use traditional music instruments such as ngoma (traditional drums), marimba, coconut shell fiddles, filimbi (whistles made of wood or bone) and Traditional trumpets.

Zanzibar is the heart of distinctive Taarab or sung poetry tradition. The Goddess of this haunting style is Siti bint Saad, the first East African Singer to make a commercial recording way back in 1928. Also inspired by Siti bint Saad, Bi. Fatuma binti Baraka (Bi Kidude), became undisputed queen of Taarab and Unyago music, and she is considered among those that have carried the music transcending generations. Notably, Taarab is the popular music in the Coastal towns of Tanzania (Dar es Salaam, Tanga, Zanzibar) and Islands of Zanzibar

Art

Tingatinga Painting

With a National pride in Swahili, novels and poetry in the language are popular and there is a strong background of literature.

The graphic art are also prevalent. The colorful and distinctive Tinga Tinga paintings (mainly of animals and birds) and the Ebony carvings of Makonde people are particularly cherished. Sculptures and carvings are also made for the important tourist industry. When you visit Zanzibar, in addition, contemporary views of stone town, carved Zanzibar Chest and Zanzibar Door are distinctive pieces of Art one must appreciate.
